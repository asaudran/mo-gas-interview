🧠 Mindset & The MO Role

What Middle Office Does Daily
  • Compute and report daily P&L to traders and management
  • Validate and reconcile trade bookings (vs front office)
  • Monitor risk limits (VaR, Delta, position limits)
  • Produce VaR, Greeks, and sensitivities reports
  • Investigate and explain unexpected P&L moves
  • Mark forward curves and verify market data
  • Coordinate between traders, risk, finance, and IT
  • Support regulatory reporting (CFTC, FERC)
  • Run scenario analysis and stress tests
  • Manage trade lifecycle events (expirations, settlements)
Key Soft Skills Interviewers Are Testing
  • Attention to detail — small errors cause large P&L misstatements
  • Commercial awareness — understand what drives gas prices today
  • Communication — explain complex risk metrics to non-experts
  • Analytical rigor — data-driven, hypothesis-driven thinking
  • Speed under pressure — MO works on tight EOD deadlines
  • Escalation instinct — know when to flag, not just fix silently
  • Collaboration — MO sits between traders and back office

Mindset mantra: "I validate, explain, and protect." MO is the control layer between trading and the world. Every number you report is a decision input. Own it.

How to Frame Your Answers

Technical Questions

Lead with the definition, then the formula/method, then real-world application, then limitations. Show you know both the theory AND where it breaks down. Interviewers in energy respect candidates who know the limitations of their models.

Market Questions

Always anchor to a fundamental framework (supply/demand), then add current-year specifics. Know 2024-2025 key events: LNG export growth, Permian associated gas surge, storage trajectory. Don't just describe — have a view.

⛽ Gas Market Fundamentals

Supply & Demand Framework

Supply Drivers
  • Dry gas production — ~105 Bcf/d in 2024 (record high)
  • Associated gas — produced with crude oil (Permian, Eagle Ford). Not price-responsive to gas prices — driven by oil economics
  • Canadian imports — ~7-8 Bcf/d via northern pipelines
  • Storage withdrawals — ~30-40 Bcf/d during winter peak
  • LNG re-gasification — minimal (US is net exporter)
  • Rig count (Baker Hughes) — leading indicator for future production
Demand Drivers
  • Power generation — largest and most volatile (~40 Bcf/d); competes with renewables
  • Residential/Commercial — highly weather-sensitive; driven by HDDs
  • Industrial — relatively stable; includes petrochemical feedstocks
  • LNG exports — ~14 Bcf/d in 2024; structural floor to US prices
  • Mexico pipeline exports — ~6-7 Bcf/d and growing
  • Storage injections — April–October; ~10-15 Bcf/d average

Forward Curve Shape — Seasonality

Typical Annual Pattern
  • Winter peak (Nov–Feb): highest prices, heating demand + storage withdrawal
  • Spring trough (Mar–Apr): demand falls, injection season begins
  • Summer shoulder (May–Jun): moderate; power burn ramps as AC demand rises
  • Late summer peak (Jul–Aug): air conditioning = power burn = gas demand in the South
  • Fall shoulder (Sep–Oct): storage nearly full, prices soft
Key Relationships to Know
  • Gas–Power nexus: High gas prices → gas-to-coal switching in power → demand destruction → price cap
  • Storage vs. Price: Storage above 5-yr avg → bearish; below → bullish
  • Spark spread: Power price – (gas price × heat rate). Indicates gas plant profitability
  • Basis = location spread: Hub X price – Henry Hub price
  • Calendar spread: Price Month A – Price Month B

Price formation summary: Storage levels set the medium-term tone. Weather sets the short-term volatility. LNG exports set the structural floor. Associated gas growth sets the ceiling pressure. Pipeline constraints create regional dislocations.

ERCOT & Nodal Pricing — Explained

What is ERCOT?

ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas) is the grid operator covering ~90% of Texas. It operates as an island — mostly disconnected from the eastern and western US grids. This means Texas electricity prices are entirely determined by Texas supply/demand dynamics.

Nodal Pricing (since 2011)

  • ~900+ settlement points (nodes) across the grid
  • Each node has its own LMP (Locational Marginal Price)
  • LMP = Energy component + Congestion component + Loss component
  • Congestion occurs when transmission lines are constrained
  • Node prices can diverge massively during stress events

Day-Ahead vs Real-Time Market

  • Day-Ahead Market (DAM): Settled the previous day; financial clearing
  • Real-Time Market (RTM): 5-minute dispatch; settles imbalances
  • Power traders manage DAM exposure + real-time basis
  • Gas-to-power relationship: gas price × heat rate = approximate power price
  • Heat rate: typically 7,000–9,500 BTU/kWh for gas plants

Gas–Power nexus in ERCOT: In summer, Texas air conditioning demand drives power prices to $100–$500/MWh during peak afternoon hours. Gas-fired plants set the marginal price ~60% of the time. A spike in gas prices directly flows through to power prices, and vice versa. The MO trader must understand both the gas forward curve AND the spark spread at key Texas hubs.

Winter Storm Uri — The Defining Texas Gas Event

Winter Storm Uri — February 10-19, 2021
Largest energy price spike in US history
What happened: An Arctic polar vortex sent temperatures in Texas to -2°F (record lows). Approximately 45 GW of generation went offline — both gas plants and wind turbines — due to freeze-offs, equipment failure, and fuel supply disruptions.

Gas price impact: Spot gas at Waha spiked to $400+/MMBtu (from ~$3 normal). Some intraday trades cleared at >$1,000/MMBtu. Henry Hub hit $23/MMBtu.

Power price impact: ERCOT real-time prices hit the administrative cap of $9,000/MWh for approximately 32 hours.

Cause of gas disruption: Wellhead freeze-offs at ~15 Bcf/d of production. Inadequate winterization of equipment. Pipeline instruments froze. Processing plants tripped offline.

Lessons for MO: Tail risk is real. VaR models based on historical data would have dramatically underestimated Uri losses. Stress scenarios must include polar vortex / infrastructure freeze events. Position limits matter — firms without limits faced massive uncapped losses.

📊 VaR & Risk Metrics

VaR Definition: Value at Risk is the maximum loss expected over a given time horizon at a given confidence level, under normal market conditions.
e.g., "1-day 99% VaR = $2M" means: there is a 1% probability that the portfolio loses more than $2M in a single day.

The Three VaR Methods

Historical Simulation

Most common in energy trading

  • Apply historical P&L scenarios (e.g., last 500 days) to current portfolio
  • No distributional assumption
  • Captures fat tails and non-linearity naturally
  • Depends on look-back period chosen
  • Misses scenarios not in history
Parametric (Delta-Normal)

Fast, analytical approach

  • Assumes normal distribution of returns
  • VaR = Position × σ × z-score (1.645 for 95%, 2.33 for 99%)
  • Fast to compute; good for linear positions
  • Underestimates tail risk (gas returns are NOT normal)
  • Poor for options (non-linear payoffs)
Monte Carlo

Most flexible, most expensive

  • Simulate thousands of price paths based on statistical model
  • Can model complex correlations and non-linearities
  • Best for complex options books
  • Computationally intensive
  • Garbage in, garbage out — model risk!

VaR Limitations — Always Mention These

  • Does not capture tail risk beyond the confidence level (the 1% can be catastrophic)
  • Assumes normal/historical conditions — breaks down in crises (Uri, 2008)
  • Look-back period bias — calm period → low VaR → false comfort
  • Correlation instability — correlations spike to 1 in crises
  • Liquidity risk ignored — VaR assumes you CAN exit at market price
  • Not sub-additive in all cases — portfolio VaR can exceed sum of parts
  • Doesn't capture model risk — the model itself can be wrong
  • Static snapshot — doesn't account for position changes during the day

Key Formulas

Parametric VaR = Position_Value × σ × z × √(holding_period)
10-day VaR ≈ 1-day VaR × √10 (≈ 3.16 × 1-day VaR)
CVaR (Expected Shortfall) = E[Loss | Loss > VaR] → average loss in the tail

CVaR / Expected Shortfall

CVaR answers: "Given that I've breached VaR, how bad is it on average?" It captures the magnitude of tail losses, not just their probability. Regulators (Basel III/IV) prefer CVaR. For gas traders, CVaR is critical — tail events like Uri are extreme AND frequent enough that the tail matters enormously.

Backtesting VaR

  • Purpose: Verify that realized losses exceed VaR with the expected frequency (e.g., ~1% of days at 99% confidence)
  • Exceptions: Days when actual loss > VaR estimate. Expected: ~2-3 exceptions/year at 99% daily VaR
  • Basel traffic light system: Green (0-4 exceptions/250 days), Yellow (5-9), Red (10+)
  • Kupiec test: Statistical test for whether exception frequency is consistent with confidence level
  • If too few exceptions: VaR is too conservative (capital inefficient)
  • If too many exceptions: VaR underestimates risk; model needs recalibration

💹 Mark-to-Market & P&L

MtM Definition: Mark-to-Market is the process of valuing open positions at current market prices (the forward curve), regardless of cost basis. The difference between current market value and book value = unrealized P&L.

P&L Attribution — Breaking Down Daily P&L

ComponentExplanationTypical Sign
New Trades P&LDay-1 P&L on trades booked today (bid-offer capture or cost)Can be + or –
Price P&L (Delta P&L)P&L from changes in forward prices × delta (position size)Depends on position
Theta (Time Decay)P&L from the passage of time — mainly affects options, storageUsually – for long options
Gamma P&LP&L from non-linear price moves (curvature of options)+ for long gamma
Vega P&LP&L from changes in implied volatility+ if long volatility
Curve P&LP&L from changes in curve shape (not parallel shifts)Depends on position
Basis P&LP&L from changes in location spreads (e.g., Waha vs. HH)Depends on position

Daily P&L Explain Process

  • Step 1: Compare today's P&L to yesterday's P&L — identify the delta
  • Step 2: Break down by product and tenor (prompt month, winter strip, summer, etc.)
  • Step 3: Map price moves on the forward curve to position Greeks
  • Step 4: Identify any "unexplained" P&L — model errors, booking errors, missing trades
  • Step 5: Reconcile with trader's own P&L estimate (traders keep their own books)
  • Step 6: Document and distribute; escalate discrepancies >threshold (e.g., $100K unexplained)

Realized vs Unrealized P&L

Realized P&L
  • P&L from trades that have been settled or expired
  • Cash has changed hands (or will shortly)
  • Locked in — cannot change
  • Drives accounting income
Unrealized (MtM) P&L
  • Open positions valued at current market prices
  • Can swing dramatically with market moves
  • Does NOT represent cash until position is closed
  • Management focus — tracks trader performance daily

↕️ Positions & Hedging Strategies

Long vs Short — The Fundamentals

Long Position

Profit if price rises. Lose if price falls.

  • Physical: Gas in storage; gas purchased forward; pipeline capacity to receive
  • Financial: Long NYMEX futures; long call options; long swap (receive floating)
  • Risk: Exposed to price decline; storage costs; time decay on options
  • Hedging: Sell futures, buy put options, enter fixed-for-floating swap as fixed receiver
Short Position

Profit if price falls. Lose if price rises.

  • Physical: Committed to deliver gas at fixed price; sold forward without owning supply
  • Financial: Short NYMEX futures; long put options; short swap (pay floating)
  • Risk: Exposed to price spike; unlimited upside risk if short and unhedged
  • Hedging: Buy futures, buy call options, enter swap as fixed payer

Basis Risk — Critical Concept

Basis risk arises when your hedge instrument doesn't perfectly match your exposure. Example: You have physical gas at Waha but hedge with NYMEX Henry Hub futures. If Waha basis widens from -$1 to -$5/MMBtu, you have a $4 unhedged loss even though HH prices were flat. Basis swaps exist specifically to hedge this risk.

Expressing the View

Bullish (expect price to rise)
  • Buy NYMEX NG futures (long delta)
  • Buy call options (limited downside, leverage upside)
  • Enter swap as floating receiver (receive floating = receive market price)
  • Long calendar spread (if bullish on near-term vs. deferred)
  • Long Waha/HH basis (if bullish on Permian takeaway improving)
Bearish (expect price to fall)
  • Sell NYMEX NG futures (short delta)
  • Buy put options (protection + speculation)
  • Enter swap as fixed receiver / floating payer
  • Short calendar spread (near vs deferred)
  • Short HSC/HH basis (if LNG disruption expected)

Interview tip: When asked "What's your view on gas markets?" — give a structured answer: (1) current storage position, (2) near-term weather, (3) structural LNG/production outlook, (4) key risk to your view. Always name the risk to your view — it shows intellectual honesty and risk awareness.

Risk Limit Framework

Typical Limits for a Gas Desk

  • Daily VaR limit — e.g., $5M at 99% 1-day
  • Delta limit — max open delta (e.g., ±100,000 MMBtu/day)
  • Stop-loss limit — e.g., -$10M cumulative P&L triggers forced review
  • Tenor/maturity limit — limit on long-dated positions (illiquid)
  • Basis limit — max open basis exposure per location
  • Stress loss limit — max loss under defined stress scenarios

MO Role in Limit Monitoring

  • Compute limits utilization at EOD (and intraday for critical limits)
  • Alert risk managers when limit is >80% utilized (yellow flag)
  • Escalate breaches immediately — do NOT wait for EOD report
  • Document all limit exceptions with trader explanation
  • Track trend: is utilization growing toward limits?

Key interview point: "How do you handle a VaR breach?" Answer: (1) Verify the data — is it a real breach or a data error? (2) Notify risk management immediately. (3) Engage the trader on cause and remediation plan. (4) Document everything. (5) Determine if temporary limit override is needed or if position must be reduced. Never sit on a breach.

💎 Value Capture & Optimization

Storage Optimization — The Core Value Machine

Gas storage value comes from the ability to buy cheap gas (summer/off-peak) and sell expensive gas (winter/peak). The theoretical value of storage = winter price – summer price – injection/withdrawal costs – carrying costs.

Intrinsic Value

  • Value from locking in current calendar spread (winter–summer)
  • Deterministic — calculated from forward curve today
  • Can be hedged immediately (sell winter, buy summer)
  • Safe, lower return — fully hedged

Extrinsic (Optionality) Value

  • Value from flexibility to inject/withdraw based on future price evolution
  • Storage is a real option — ability to respond to price changes
  • Higher potential return if market is volatile
  • Requires active trading and risk management
  • Valued using option pricing models (binomial, Monte Carlo)

Other Value Capture Strategies

Geographic Arbitrage
  • Buy gas at Waha (cheap), transport to HSC (expensive)
  • Value = HSC – Waha – transport tariff
  • Requires pipeline capacity (firm or interruptible)
  • Waha discount creates persistent arb opportunities
Calendar Spread Trading
  • Trade the spread between two delivery months
  • e.g., Long Jan – Short Apr (long winter/short spring)
  • Profit from relative supply/demand shifts
  • Less directional risk than outright positions
Swing / Flexibility Options
  • Contract gives flexibility in volumes taken
  • Value = premium for flexibility vs. firm contract
  • Common in industrial supply contracts
  • Valued via Monte Carlo over delivery period

Key MO insight: The MO role in value capture is to accurately measure whether the trading strategy is capturing the expected value. This means tracking realized calendar spread vs. theoretical storage value, monitoring transport margin, and flagging when the book is underperforming vs. the model.

📋 US Gas Case Studies

Case 1 — Winter Storm Uri: February 2021
The Black Swan That Wasn't
Context: Polar vortex delivers record cold to Texas. ~45 GW of generation offline. ~15 Bcf/d of gas production frozen at the wellhead.

Price action: Spot Waha gas → $400+/MMBtu. Henry Hub → $23/MMBtu. ERCOT power → $9,000/MWh cap for 32+ hours.

What went wrong: Gas wells, pipelines, and power plant instruments lacked winterization. ERCOT's island grid had no emergency imports available. Gas-fired plants couldn't get fuel; wind turbines froze.

Risk management lesson: Historical VaR based on 2017-2020 data = near zero tail risk for this scenario. It wasn't in the data. Reverse stress testing and hypothetical scenario analysis would have flagged this. Firms without extreme weather stress scenarios were unprepared.

Aftermath: Texas passed SB 3 (2021) requiring weatherization of power plants and gas wells. ERCOT reformed its market design. Ongoing debate about adequacy payments.
Case 2 — Freeport LNG Explosion: June 2022
LNG Export Outage → Domestic Price Crash
Context: Freeport LNG (Quintana Island, TX) — one of the largest LNG export terminals in the US at ~2 Bcf/d — suffered a pipeline explosion and fire on June 8, 2022.

Immediate price action: HH prices fell ~$2/MMBtu overnight (from ~$9 to ~$7). The market immediately priced in ~2 Bcf/d of demand being removed from the domestic market.

Duration: Terminal was offline for ~90 days (restarted partially in October 2022).

Key takeaway: LNG exports are now a structural part of US gas demand. Any disruption to export capacity immediately affects domestic prices — and the relationship is inverse: LNG outage = domestic price bearish. This is the opposite of traditional supply disruptions.

MO relevance: Positions with LNG exposure (HSC basis, forward gas) required immediate re-marking and stress test updates. Risk teams had to rapidly assess which books were long LNG exposure vs. domestic.
Case 3 — Permian Waha Basis Collapse: 2019
Associated Gas Overwhelms Pipeline Takeaway
Context: Permian Basin oil production boomed in 2018-2019. Associated gas production rose ~10 Bcf/d. Pipeline takeaway capacity out of the Permian had NOT kept pace with production growth.

Price action: Waha Hub basis collapsed from roughly -$0.50 to -$9/MMBtu in mid-2019. Prices turned briefly negative (producers paid to get rid of gas, as they couldn't shut wells — they were drilling for oil).

Why it matters: This is a structural feature of Permian gas economics: oil producers drill regardless of gas prices. Gas is a by-product. When pipes are full, prices crash. New pipelines (PHP, Whistler) eventually resolved the constraint.

Trading lesson: Waha basis is a key risk factor for any Texas gas book. It can move 5-10× faster than HH. Basis swaps are essential for Permian exposure. Any firm with unhedged Waha exposure in 2019 suffered massive losses.
Case 4 — US LNG Export Boom & HH Surge: 2022
Russia's Ukraine Invasion Reshapes Global Gas
Context: Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022. Europe scrambled to replace Russian pipeline gas with US LNG. US LNG exporters ran at maximum capacity. European TTF prices hit €300/MWh equivalent.

Henry Hub impact: HH reached $9.70/MMBtu in August 2022 — its highest level in 14 years. The LNG pull was the dominant driver alongside hot summer temperatures.

Key mechanics: When TTF (European gas) >> HH × liquefaction cost → all US LNG goes to Europe → US domestic supply tightens → HH rises. This arbitrage linkage is now permanent given US export capacity.

LNG netback pricing: HH should theoretically = TTF – liquefaction cost (~$3/MMBtu) – shipping (~$1-3/MMBtu). If HH is below this, more cargoes divert to Europe.

2024 onwards: With LNG export capacity at ~14 Bcf/d and growing (new terminals under construction), HH has a structural floor set by global LNG markets.
Case 5 — COVID Demand Destruction: 2020
When Demand Collapsed Faster Than Supply Could Adjust
Context: COVID-19 lockdowns in spring 2020 crushed industrial and commercial gas demand globally. US gas production continued at elevated levels, while LNG demand also fell (global shipping disruption + lower LNG demand in Asia).

Price action: HH fell to $1.40-$1.50/MMBtu in mid-2020 — near the lowest level in 25 years. Storage quickly filled to near-record levels.

Recovery: Production eventually curtailed as rigs dropped. LNG exports partially recovered. HH recovered to ~$3 by late 2020.

Risk management takeaway: Demand shocks can be sudden and extreme. Scenarios should include rapid demand destruction events. 2020 also demonstrated that even near-record low prices don't quickly reduce supply when many wells are already drilled and low-cost to operate.

🔌 ERCOT Market Structure

What is ERCOT?
  • Electric Reliability Council of Texas — ISO/RTO operating ~90% of Texas grid
  • ~26 million customers, ~85 GW of installed capacity
  • Islanded grid — minimal AC connections to Eastern/Western US interconnections (only ~1,100 MW DC ties)
  • Consequence: no emergency imports from neighbors — Texas prices are 100% set by Texas supply/demand
  • Regulated by the Public Utility Commission of Texas (PUCT), not FERC
  • Unique in the US: competitive wholesale market with no capacity market
Market Design — Energy-Only
  • ERCOT is an energy-only market — no separate capacity payments (unlike PJM, MISO, ISO-NE)
  • Generators recover all fixed costs through energy prices
  • Result: prices can spike to very high levels during scarcity (this is by design)
  • Administrative price cap: $5,000/MWh (HCAP) up to $9,000/MWh (VOLL) during emergencies
  • ORDC (Operating Reserve Demand Curve) adder: when reserves are tight, an adder is applied to real-time price
  • Key debate: should Texas add a capacity market? (ongoing PUCT discussion)

📍 Nodal Pricing & LMP

LMP = Locational Marginal Price = the cost of serving an additional MWh at a specific node. It breaks down into:
LMP = Energy Component + Congestion Component + Loss Component

Energy Component
  • The system-wide marginal cost (lambda)
  • Same for all nodes when no congestion
  • Set by the most expensive dispatched generator
  • Gas plants often set the marginal price (~60% of hours)
Congestion Component
  • Arises when transmission lines hit their capacity limit
  • Shadow price of the binding constraint
  • Can be positive or negative at different nodes
  • Downstream of a constraint → higher price; upstream → lower
  • Creates massive intra-day price divergence between nodes
Loss Component
  • Marginal cost of electrical losses in transmission
  • Nodes far from load centers pay a loss "penalty"
  • Relatively small vs. energy and congestion
  • Can be positive or negative depending on location

Financial Transmission Rights (FTRs)

FTRs (called CRRs — Congestion Revenue Rights — in ERCOT) are financial instruments that entitle the holder to a stream of revenue (or obligation) based on the congestion component of LMPs between two nodes. They are used to hedge congestion risk. A generator at Node A selling power at Node B buys an FTR from A→B to lock in the congestion price. FTR value = (LMP_B – LMP_A) × MWh. FTRs are auctioned periodically by ERCOT.

⚡ Power Market Fundamentals

Supply Stack (Merit Order)

The merit order determines which plants run. ERCOT dispatches cheapest available generation first. The price is set by the most expensive unit needed to meet demand (marginal unit).

Key Texas Power Demand Drivers

Summer (Peak Season)
  • Air conditioning is the dominant driver — Texas summer = aggressive AC
  • Peak demand typically 70-85 GW on hottest days (vs ~30 GW overnight)
  • CDD (Cooling Degree Days) key metric: each degree above 65°F adds ~1 GW demand
  • Peak hour typically 3-6 PM when solar begins to ramp down and AC is full
  • Weather-driven price spikes of $500-$9,000/MWh on extreme days
Winter (Growing Risk)
  • Post-Uri, winter is now a serious risk season for ERCOT
  • Electric heating demand grows each year (less gas heating, more heat pumps)
  • HDDs drive heating demand; polar vortex = massive sudden spike
  • Solar generation minimal in winter; wind less reliable during freeze
  • Storage (batteries) playing growing role in winter reliability

Renewable Integration in ERCOT

ERCOT has the most wind capacity of any US grid (~40 GW) and rapidly growing solar (~20+ GW). This creates a "duck curve" problem: solar generation mid-day depresses prices, then ramps down sharply in late afternoon just as AC demand peaks — requiring fast-ramping gas plants (CCGT, OCGT) to fill the gap. Negative prices now occur in West Texas during windy/sunny low-demand periods (wind curtailment zone). This has major implications for gas-to-power relationships and spark spread trading.

🔥 Spark Spread & Gas-Power Nexus

Spark Spread = Power Price ($/MWh) – [Gas Price ($/MMBtu) × Heat Rate (MMBtu/MWh)]
Measures the gross margin of generating electricity from natural gas. Positive = profitable to run the plant. Negative = plant shuts down (if variable cost exceeds power price).

Spark Spread = P_power – (P_gas × HR) Example: $80/MWh – ($3.00/MMBtu × 8.0 MMBtu/MWh) = $80 – $24 = $56/MWh gross margin
CCGT (Efficient)
  • Heat rate: 6,500–7,500 BTU/kWh
  • Runs most hours — competitive against wind/coal
  • Large positive spark spreads available in summer peaks
  • At $3 gas: breakeven power ~$20-22/MWh
OCGT Peaker
  • Heat rate: 9,000–12,000 BTU/kWh
  • Only economic at high power prices
  • Runs <5% of hours; sets marginal price on hot days
  • At $3 gas: breakeven power ~$27-36/MWh
Clean Spark Spread
  • Adjusts for carbon cost (if applicable)
  • = Spark Spread – (CO2_intensity × carbon_price)
  • Less relevant in Texas (no carbon price currently)
  • Important for firms with global portfolios

💱 Power Trading Instruments

Financial Products
  • ERCOT Hub Forward (ICE) — most liquid; ERCOT HUB or zone; on-peak / off-peak / flat
  • On-peak block — Hours Ending 7-22 Monday–Friday (16 hours/day)
  • Off-peak block — all other hours (nights, weekends)
  • Spark spread swap — buys or sells the spark spread directly; separates power and gas risk
  • Heat rate option — option on the spark spread; used for peaker asset optimization
  • CRR/FTR — hedges nodal congestion risk
Physical / Operational
  • Day-ahead energy bids — submit offer stack into DAM by 10 AM
  • Real-time dispatch — ERCOT dispatches units via SCED every 5 minutes
  • Ancillary services — Reg Up/Down, RRS, ECRS, Non-Spin; separate revenue stream
  • Tolling agreement — right to dispatch a third-party plant; pay fuel + fixed fee, receive power
  • PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) — long-term fixed-price contracts for renewable offtake

P&L Attribution for Power

  • Energy P&L — flat price move on net delta position (hub price × MWh delta)
  • Congestion P&L — nodal price vs. hub divergence on physical positions
  • Spark Spread P&L — change in gas-to-power ratio; occurs when gas and power move differently
  • Ancillary Revenue — settlement of ancillary service awards; reconciled vs. Day-Ahead awards
  • Shape P&L — difference between actual hourly prices and block position (on-peak vs. all hours)
  • Settlement P&L — DAM vs. RTM settlement differences (imbalance charges)
